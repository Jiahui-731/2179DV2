Can We Swim Today?

Every summer, millions of Melburnians flock to the beaches of Port Phillip Bay. But the water isn't always as clean as it looks. Using six years of EPA Victoria sampling and Bureau of Meteorology rainfall data, this is a story about when, where, and why it's actually safe to swim.

By Jiahui · 5,416 routine samples · 36 beaches · 2020-2025

Three things the data already knows

  1. Across 5,416 routine samples, 10.2% fail EPA's swim-safe threshold — but the failures cluster, badly.
  2. The single biggest predictor isn't the season but yesterday's rain: more than 20mm nearly doubles the share of failed samples.
  3. Eastern Bayside beaches got dramatically dirtier between 2020-2022 and 2023-2025; several Inner-North beaches actually improved.

① Where

Where are the monitored beaches?

Twenty kilometres south of Melbourne, Port Phillip Bay arcs in a near-circle — a 1,930-square-kilometre tidal basin almost sealed off from Bass Strait by the thin curl of the Mornington Peninsula. Since 2013, EPA Victoria has tracked bacteria levels at 36 beaches ringing its shore. Hover over any dot to read its long-term average.

That geography is hard to read at a glance — the bay is shaped less like a circle than a slightly squashed teardrop. So next, take the same 36 beaches and unfold the coastline into a perfect ring: each ray, one beach, clockwise from Williamstown at the top. The longer the ray, the dirtier the beach.

② How dirty

Not every beach is created equal.

Bacteria levels — measured as enterococci, the bug that signals fecal contamination — vary widely between beaches. Frankston Coast Guard averages 439 organisms per 100mL across routine samples; Portsea, just down the peninsula, averages 13. Take the same beach order as the radial chart above and unfold it left-to-right — the bay's pollution skyline emerges as bars.

Zoom in on the worst end of the bay's hierarchy. Ten beaches — most of them along the Inner-North and Eastern Bayside corridor — sit far above the EPA Poor threshold, with long-term averages above 100 organisms / 100 mL. Frankston Coast Guard tops the list at 439.

③ When

The dirtiest months are the ones we swim in.

Bacteria don't bloom uniformly across the year. The ten beaches with the highest long-term averages — the bay's worst — show a clear seasonal pattern: a red band runs from late summer through early autumn, the same months people swim.

Zoom out from months to years and a different question opens up: which beaches are changing? Of the 36 monitored sites, ten moved by a meaningful margin — five for the worse, five for the better. Each row pairs a beach's 2020-2022 average (hollow) with its 2023-2025 average (solid).

Some months are bay-wide disasters. The grid below collapses six years onto a 12-month calendar: each cell is one specific month. Four cells run deep red — November 2022, November 2025, April 2024, and December 2024 — when more than a quarter of bay-wide samples failed; in the first three, nearly one in three.

④ Why

The single biggest predictor isn't the season.

It's whether it rained yesterday. Stormwater runoff carries pet waste, fertilizer and street pollution into the bay; bacteria spike within 24 hours. Below, every dot is one sample, plotted against the previous day's rainfall (Melbourne Olympic Park gauge). The dashed line is the trend.

Bin the data by yesterday's rainfall and the effect is stark.

⑤ Region

Geography beats timing.

Group the bay into four regions and one wins by a wide margin: the Western Shore — Werribee, Geelong, Portarlington — has roughly half the bacteria of the urban Inner North. People assume the Mornington Peninsula is the bay's clean haven; the data says the opposite.

⑥ Verdict

So — should you swim today?

The data answers most of the question for you. Find today's month across the top. Find yesterday's rainfall down the side. The cell where they meet is the share of historical routine samples in that region that came back Poor under those exact conditions.

The summary is simple: avoid the inner-city beaches; avoid the 24 hours after heavy rain; avoid late summer and early autumn if you can. Otherwise — go swim.

A note on method

Detection limits. EPA reports values below 10 orgs/100mL as "<10". These censored values are recoded to 5 — the standard half-detection-limit convention for environmental bacterial data. When EPA reports a different limit, the row uses half of that reported limit.

Site renumbering. The Portarlington monitoring site moved a short distance in October 2022 (EPA ID 99760 → 99761). The two IDs are merged here for time-series continuity.

Rainfall. All rainfall figures come from the BOM Melbourne Olympic Park gauge — the closest reliable long-record station to the bay's eastern shore. Western-shore beaches may experience slightly different precipitation; this is a known approximation, used because no single station covers the whole bay.

Routine samples. Headline summaries use scheduled Routine samples only, so EPA follow-up resamples do not double-count known incidents.

Safety thresholds. Good ≤ 35, Caution 35-200, Poor > 200 organisms / 100mL — EPA Beach Action Values on a single-sample basis.

Chart 04 scaling. The rainfall scatter caps its y-axis at 1,000 to keep the trend line readable. About 2% of samples sit above this; they are off-chart visually but still counted in the regression.

Code & data. All chart specifications (.vg.json), the preprocessing script, and the source CSVs are open in the project repository.
